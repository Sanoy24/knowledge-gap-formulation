## Question - Day 3

In my Week 11 Tenacious-Bench judge training (`training/train_judge.py`), I trained a SimPO LoRA adapter on Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct with rank 16, β=2.0, γ/β=0.75, on 618 preference pairs. The run finished at `training_loss = 0.016`. On the 100-pair dev subset that `compute_pairwise_accuracy` scores, base accuracy is 98% (98/100), trained is 100% (100/100), a net 2 pairs flipped. On the sealed 44-pair held-out, base is 97.73% (43/44), trained is 100% (44/44), +2.27pp at p=0.372. The first 100 dev pairs contain 18 adversarial pairs (9 P007, 3 P011, 6 P027). Nothing in what I logged tells me whether those flips landed on the 18 cases I built the bench to stress, or on the 82 easy ones I do not particularly care about.

**Across my SimPO training run, what fraction of the 618 preference pairs had a base-model length-normalized log-prob margin already above γ before the first gradient step, and for the pairs that started below γ (the only ones carrying any real learning signal), what was the per-step gradient trajectory before they too saturated and went silent? The answer determines whether my +2.27pp held-out lift came from training touching the adversarial cases I built the bench for, or from cleaning up incidental easy pairs while the adversarial set stayed exactly as the base model already had it.**

A satisfying answer needs to do three things. First, give me the per-pair SimPO gradient `∂L/∂θ` as a function of margin slack `m = β·M − γ` and show how fast it decays once a pair crosses γ. Second, tell me what training dynamics look like in two opposite cases: real learning on a thin hard set, versus loss that saturates immediately because most pairs started above γ. Third, settle which engineering move actually changes the regime my run sits in. The candidates are raising γ to re-engage saturated pairs, hard-pair mining the residual, or giving up on tuning the model and instead expanding the adversarial held-out partition so a real lift becomes detectable above noise.

## Sharpening Responses (from Amir's interrogation)

**Q: Your baseline pairwise accuracy is 98%, not 97.7% — and training moved it to 100%. That's 2 pairs flipped out of 100 evaluated. Do you actually know WHICH 2 pairs the base model got wrong?**

You're right about the number. The dev figure is 98%; the 97.73% is held-out at n=44. On dev, base is 98/100 and trained is 100/100, so the **net** move is 2 wrong→right flips. Without per-pair logging I cannot rule out that some pair regressed right→wrong with extra wrong→right flips offsetting it. The minimum is 2 flips. Same on held-out: minimum 1 flip, possibly more. Whatever the composition, the question collapses to "what pairs is base Qwen2.5-3B actually wrong on, and were any of them adversarial?" That is what the diagnostic script (`ablations/diagnose_per_pair_margins.py`) resolves in 15 minutes by surfacing the task_ids and probe labels of the flipped pairs.

**Q: Your `compute_pairwise_accuracy` evaluates only the first 100 dev pairs (`DEV_EVAL_LIMIT=100`). You have 347 dev pairs. Are P007/P011/P027 even in those first 100?**

Yes, and I have the count. Of 347 total dev pairs, the first 100 contain 9 with P007, 3 with P011, 6 with P027. 18 adversarial pairs in the eval window. Sample task_ids: `tb-trace-p011-012`, `tb-trace-p012-052`, `tb-prog-weo-005`. Verified by running PowerShell against `training_data/dev.jsonl`. This actually tightens your #1 rather than relaxing it. If 18 of 100 eval pairs are adversarial and at minimum 2 pairs flipped, the strongest reading is that training fixed adversarial pairs the base model missed. The weakest is that it fixed easy ones and left every adversarial case exactly as the base had it. The script tells me which.

**Q: In part (a), you're asking about gradient magnitude decay — but your training loss is 0.016. The gradient story is already told: by epoch 2, every pair sits far above γ and the gradient is near-zero. Is your real question "why did I train for 2 epochs when 1 was probably enough"?**

Conceded, and the concession reshapes the question instead of killing it. SimPO loss `−log σ(β·M − γ)` reaches 0.016 on average when β·M − γ is large on essentially every pair. My run is saturated by end of training. The "what is the gradient doing in the final epoch" framing assumed a regime I was already past. Your reframe is what I actually want: were the hard pairs ever unsaturated long enough to produce meaningful gradient, or did the easy pairs drag the average loss to zero before the hard ones got real updates? The 0.016 endpoint is consistent with two opposite stories. Hard pairs got real gradient and then saturated, or hard pairs were too few to matter and the run was mostly a no-op. The endpoint cannot distinguish them. The trajectory can.

**Q: For part (b), how many of your 618 pairs have base-model margin BELOW γ=1.5? If it's 5–10 out of 618, you'd be training on a dataset so small that gradient noise dominates signal.**

I can bound this without running the script. Two indirect arguments. First, training dynamics: my run completed in 2 epochs at effective batch 8, which is about 150 gradient steps on 618 pairs. Reaching `final_pairwise_acc = 1.0` and loss 0.016 inside those 150 steps tells me the optimizer had small work to do, not large work. A run with hundreds of below-γ pairs would not converge that fast at LR 5e-5. So the count plausibly sits in the low tens. The word "plausibly" is doing real work in that sentence. Second, base-model strength: Qwen2.5-3B gets 98/100 on dev pairwise accuracy. Extrapolating from dev to the 618 train pairs (same source distribution), roughly 12 pairs probably have negative base margin. The count between 0 and γ is unconstrained by the accuracy number but bounded by the saturation argument. So the hard set is plausibly small. Training had real signal on a thin slice. The loss saturated because the slice was thin. Your reframe of (b) ("you are not breaking the reference-free assumption, you just have too few pairs for stable training signal") is consistent with both arguments. The histogram confirms the exact count.

**Q: Your question bundles three sub-questions (a, b, c) that have different answer types. Which one do you want the explainer to spend 600–1,000 words on?**

Pick (a), reframed. Given your #3 and #4, (b) and (c) collapse into consequences. On (b), the "hard-pair mining principle" question answers itself given the small residual count: principled in theory, useless in practice for my dataset size. The reference-free assumption is not the constraint. The residual count is. On (c), the "engineering decision" question also follows. Raising γ does re-engage previously saturated pairs because it shrinks β·M − γ and brings the gradient back from zero. What it doesn't do is manufacture new hard pairs. It just unsaturates more of the same ones. Hard-pair mining is the same operation viewed from the data side. Both moves shuffle the same small residual without growing it. The only move that changes the regime is expanding the adversarial held-out partition until any real lift becomes detectable at n > 44. So the explainer answers reframed (a). (b) and (c) become two paragraphs at the end pointing at "expand the adversarial held-out" as the recommendation.

**Q: Have you considered that the real problem isn't the gradient math but your DATA? Your `build_log.json` shows large failure rates on the chosen side. If many of your 618 surviving pairs have chosen outputs that barely pass `scoring_evaluator.py`, the preference signal is weak regardless of γ.**

Less acute for my data than for yours, and the existing build log already tells me how acute. From `training_data/build_log.json`: 1,478 chosen-rewrite attempts, 965 accepted (passed `scoring_evaluator.py`), 480 failed (32.5% drop rate), 33 skipped. The 965 accepted became my final 618 train + 347 dev = 965 pairs. Every chosen output in my training set strictly passes `passed_all_checks: true`. Unlike your template-based pipeline, my chosen side is gated by the same evaluator that scores the held-out, so the marginal-quality failure mode you flagged does not apply to my data. Your deeper point survives. Even when chosen and rejected are clearly distinguishable to the evaluator, the *log-prob signal* between them under the base model can still be weak if the model assigns them similar probabilities. That is exactly what the margin-distribution argument in #4 measures. So #6 reduces to #4 for my repo. The right diagnostic is the histogram, not a chosen-side quality re-audit.

## Why This Matters Beyond My Work

This is the same regime any FDE will hit when they train a preference judge on a narrow domain where the base model is already strong. Aggregate metrics move slightly. There is no per-pair logging to tell whether the move was on the cases that mattered. There is no base-model margin distribution to tell whether the trainer had any signal to learn from in the first place. The instinct is to reach for gradient-level fixes: raise γ, switch algorithm, increase rank. That is the wrong order. The first question is whether training had a hard set to work on at all, and the way to answer that is two scripts that should ship with any production preference-tuning run: a per-pair flip diff, and a base-model margin histogram. Neither is in my Week 11 artifacts right now. Most "marginal but not significant" lift results in small-data preference training are saturation artifacts dressed up as training success.
